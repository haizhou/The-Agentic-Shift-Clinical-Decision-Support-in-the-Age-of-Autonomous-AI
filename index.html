What If Every Student Had a Brilliant Tutor Available 24/7?

Inside the agentic AI systems that are turning Bloom’s impossible dream (personalised one-on-one tutoring for every learner) into an engineering problem with a real solution.

20 min read ~5,000 words Emerging Topics in Integrated Machine Learning Systems 25/26
Part 1

Education at a Crossroads

TL;DR

The world needs 44 million more teachers by 2030. Bloom proved one-on-one tutoring works forty years ago, but we could never afford to deliver it at scale. Then agentic AI entered the picture. Khanmigo has already reached 1.4M users, and a systematic review of 82 studies confirms: hybrid human-AI approaches outperform fully autonomous ones.

1.1 A Teacher Shortage That Keeps Getting Worse

Here is a number that should alarm you: the world needs approximately 44 million additional teachers by 2030 just to achieve universal basic education (UNESCO, 2024). That is not a projection about some distant future — it is six years away, and we are nowhere close to being on track.

The pipeline is collapsing from the other end, too. The 2024 TALIS survey found that one in five teachers under 30 plans to leave the profession within five years; in some countries, that figure rises to nearly half (Education International, 2025). In the United States alone, roughly 411,500 teaching positions were either unfilled or occupied by under-qualified staff in the 2024–25 school year, roughly one in eight positions nationally (Learning Policy Institute, 2025). These are not abstract statistics. They translate directly into overcrowded classrooms, widening achievement gaps, and students in STEM subjects who may never encounter a fully qualified teacher.

The pattern is self-reinforcing: burnout drives experienced educators out, which increases the workload on those who remain, which drives more burnout. Across OECD countries, secondary schools consistently suffer worse vacancies than primary, especially in science, mathematics, and technology (OECD Education at a Glance, 2025). Rural and economically disadvantaged communities bear the heaviest burden: limited tax bases, geographic isolation, and distance from teacher-training programmes compound recruitment challenges. In Sub-Saharan Africa, the student-to-teacher ratio can exceed 56:1 (UNESCO, 2024). At that density, personalised instruction is not merely difficult; it is physically impossible.

The numbers crystallise into something almost surreal when placed side by side. In the United States, roughly one in eight teaching positions was either unfilled or occupied by under-qualified staff in the 2024–25 school year — not in struggling rural districts alone, but nationally (Learning Policy Institute, 2025). In Sub-Saharan Africa, where the ratio can exceed 56 students to every one teacher, the very idea of individual attention collapses under its own arithmetic. These are not parallel crises. They are the same crisis, playing out at different latitudes.

1.2 MOOCs Promised a Revolution. They Delivered a Library Card.

We have been here before. MOOCs burst onto the scene in the early 2010s with world-changing ambitions: top-university lectures, free, for everyone with an internet connection. The reality was more modest. Dropout rates were staggering, and the experience was fundamentally passive: watching videos and clicking through quizzes is not the same as learning. Adaptive platforms like early Khan Academy and Carnegie Learning added a layer of personalisation through item-response algorithms, but they remained confined to predefined content paths and rigid rule-based logic. They could make a problem slightly harder if you got the last one right. They could not ask you why you got it wrong.

“Students receiving individual tutoring performed two standard deviations above conventionally taught peers — the so-called ‘two-sigma problem.’”

Benjamin Bloom, 1984

The gap between what we know works and what we can afford to deliver has haunted education for four decades. In 1984, Benjamin Bloom demonstrated that students receiving individual tutoring performed two standard deviations above conventionally taught peers. The result was so striking that it became known as the “two-sigma problem”: we had proof that one-on-one tutoring was transformative, but no way to give every student their own tutor. What was needed was a technology that could understand natural language, reason about misconceptions, and adapt its teaching strategy on the fly. For forty years, nothing came close. Then large language models arrived.

1.3 Enter Agentic AI: From Chatbot to Pedagogical Partner

The difference between a chatbot and an agentic AI is the difference between a search engine and a research assistant. A chatbot responds to queries; an agentic system pursues goals. It plans, reasons, uses tools, and adapts its approach based on what is working. In education, that means a tutor that does not just answer your question. It notices you are confused, traces the confusion to a specific misconception, retrieves a relevant example from your textbook, and walks you through it with Socratic questions (Schneider, 2025; Acharya et al., 2025).

This is not theoretical. Khan Academy’s Khanmigo, built on GPT-4, grew from roughly 68,000 users during its 2023–24 pilot to over 700,000 in 2024–25, with projections exceeding one million for 2025–26 (Khan Academy, 2025; DiCerbo, 2025). A 2025 systematic review covering 82 peer-reviewed studies distilled the field into five recurring themes: RAG dramatically reduces hallucination; prompt-engineering guardrails preserve academic integrity; multi-agent debate improves accuracy on complex tasks; affective scaffolding increases student persistence; and — perhaps most importantly — hybrid human–AI workflows consistently outperform fully autonomous tutors (Garcia-Bosque et al., 2025). The question is no longer whether AI can enhance education, but how to design, deploy, and govern it responsibly.

Part 2

The Tech Stack Behind the Intelligent Classroom

TL;DR

Building a tutor that actually works is a sequence of engineering problems, each one exposed by solving the last. How does the AI reason carefully rather than guess? How do you stop it making things up — and guarantee the maths is right? How does it communicate in a way students can actually follow? How does it remember a specific student across months? How do multiple specialised roles coordinate? And how does any of this run fast enough to feel like a conversation? Each question has an answer. Together, they add up to something that did not exist five years ago.

2.1 How Should an AI Think? From Chain-of-Thought to Thinking Time

The most obvious failure mode of an early AI tutor is not that it lacks knowledge — it is that it does not think. Ask a standard language model a physics problem and it produces an answer in a single forward pass, with the same computational budget whether the question is trivial or graduate-level. The confidence of the output tells you nothing about its correctness. This is the opposite of how a good teacher works.

Chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2023) was the first crack in this wall: prompt the model to reason step by step before committing, and performance on mathematics and logic improves substantially. The ReAct framework (Yao et al., 2023) went further — interleaving reasoning with tool use so the agent can pause mid-thought to query a knowledge base or run a calculation, then resume with that result in hand. Both were improvements. But they still operated within a fixed inference budget, allocating the same compute to every query regardless of difficulty.

The deeper shift came with test-time compute scaling. OpenAI’s o1 and o3 demonstrated something that upended the conventional wisdom: a model does not need to be bigger to be smarter — it needs more time to think. Performance scales smoothly with compute allocated at inference, not just with parameter count. A smaller model given a longer reasoning budget can outperform one fourteen times its size running standard decoding (Snell et al., ICLR 2025). For a tutor, this changes the design question entirely. Vocabulary recall needs a fraction of a second. A calculus proof benefits from extended internal deliberation. The system can now allocate thinking time dynamically, matching the depth of reasoning to the actual difficulty of the question — exactly as an experienced teacher would.

2.2 Can We Trust the Answer? Grounding Facts and Guaranteeing Correctness

Good reasoning applied to the wrong sources produces confident nonsense. A model trained on the entire internet has no particular reason to prefer your department’s lecture notes over a decade-old Reddit thread. And even when the sources are right, the model’s reasoning over them may produce plausible-looking mathematics that is subtly wrong. This section is about two complementary mechanisms for making answers actually trustworthy: retrieval grounding for factual accuracy, and neurosymbolic verification for computational correctness.

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) fixes the sourcing problem by fetching verified passages from a curated corpus before the model speaks, constraining generation to what your course actually says. But early dense-vector RAG had a structural flaw in education: it retrieved semantically similar text without understanding prerequisite dependencies. A student asking about Newton’s second law might receive a perfectly accurate passage about force — but without the scaffolding of momentum and rate of change, the answer lands without the cognitive architecture needed to make sense of it.

GraphRAG addresses this by traversing a structured concept network rather than a flat document store. If a student struggles with \(F=ma\), the knowledge graph maps this node to Mass, Acceleration, back to Velocity, and further to Momentum (dp/dt) — returning not just the answer, but the entire scaffold needed to reach it. In practice, a two-stage retrieval pipeline sharpens this further: BM25 sparse retrieval casts a wide net in milliseconds; a cross-encoder reranker then scores every candidate with full attention, surfacing the passages that are genuinely most relevant rather than merely most similar. LPITutor (Verma, 2025) extends this with a dedicated intent-extraction stage, correctly parsing a confused student’s question before deciding what to fetch.

$$ P(y \mid x) = \sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}} P(z \mid x)\, P(y \mid z, x) $$

The RAG objective: marginalise over retrieved documents \(z\). The retrieval distribution \(P(z \mid x)\) is computed via dense vector similarity; the generation distribution \(P(y \mid z, x)\) is conditioned on both query and retrieved context. The two-stage pipeline (BM25 recall → cross-encoder reranking) tightens the precision of \(z\) before it reaches the language model.

Retrieval solves the sourcing problem. It does not solve the calculation problem. Even when the retrieved passage is correct, the model’s reasoning over it may produce mathematics that looks right but is not. For a student who cannot yet verify the answer, this is the core failure. Neurosymbolic reasoning addresses this with a clear division of labour: the LLM handles intent parsing, pedagogical framing, and natural-language explanation; the symbolic solver handles deterministic, verifiable computation. SymCode (2025) operationalises this directly — translating a student’s problem into Python and SymPy, executing it, and feeding any errors back into a self-debugging loop until the result is guaranteed correct by execution, not by the model’s confidence. At the research frontier, LIPS (ICLR 2025) demonstrates the same principle on Olympiad-level inequalities: partition the tactic space so enumerable algebraic lemmas go to symbolic modules and open-ended rewriting goes to the LLM, achieving state-of-the-art results without additional training data.

RAG ensures the tutor draws on your course materials. Neurosymbolic reasoning ensures the answer is actually correct. Both layers are necessary. Neither is sufficient on its own.

2.3 How Should the Answer Be Expressed? The Multimodal Layer

A correct, well-grounded answer can still fail a student if it arrives in the wrong form. Mathematics expressed only as text is hard to follow. A physics concept that exists only as an equation never becomes intuitive. And for younger learners or those with typing difficulties, a text-only interface is a barrier before the teaching even begins. Getting the content right is not the same as getting the communication right.

Foundation models like GPT-4o, Gemini 2.5, and Claude now process text, speech, and vision natively — which means the tutoring interface can hear a student’s spoken question, see the diagram they are pointing at, and respond in kind. Researchers at the NSF iSAT Institute have fine-tuned a Discrete Multimodal LLM specifically for classroom speech, tackling the real challenges that standard ASR handles poorly: background noise, children’s pronunciation patterns, and the accents of a genuinely diverse student population (Trinh et al., 2025).

But the more consequential development is not perception — it is generation. The most effective agentic tutors are beginning to produce interactive environments on the fly: not diagrams to look at, but simulations to manipulate. The student does not read about the Ideal Gas Law; they compress the gas themselves, watch the pressure climb, and arrive at \(PV=nRT\) through their own hands. This shifts the tutor’s role from explaining to creating the conditions for understanding — a distinction that turns out to matter enormously for retention.

2.4 Does It Remember You? Persistent Memory Across Sessions

A tutor that reasons carefully, draws on the right sources, and communicates well will still fail if it forgets everything between sessions. The student makes real progress on Monday. They return on Thursday to find the system has no memory of Monday at all. Every session restarts from zero. This is not a minor inconvenience — it makes genuine personalisation impossible. A good teacher is defined, in large part, by the accumulated understanding of a specific person built over time.

The challenge is architectural. LLMs operate within a fixed context window; anything outside it is simply gone. Early workarounds prepended a summary of past sessions, but this degrades quickly as weeks of interaction compress into a lossy paragraph that loses precisely the fine-grained detail personalisation requires. The more principled approach, emerging actively from 2025–2026 research, treats the LLM as an operating system managing its own tiered memory.

MemGPT (Packer et al., 2023; now Letta) introduced the core insight: the model itself decides what to retain, summarise, and discard, using tool calls to move information between a fast in-context working memory and a slower external archival store. More recent architectures extend this considerably. MIRIX (2025) separates memory into six distinct types — Core, Episodic, Semantic, Procedural, Resource, and Knowledge Vault — each managed by a dedicated sub-agent under a meta memory controller, yielding up to 26% improvement on conversational benchmarks with more than 90% reduction in token costs compared to naive full-context approaches.

In educational terms, this is the student model that the Intelligent Tutoring Systems literature has theorised for decades — now implementable at meaningful scale. A persistent-memory tutor knows that this student consistently confuses correlation with causation, has struggled with integration by parts across three separate weeks, and responds better to worked examples than to abstract definitions. It can act on all of that next session. The gap between a chatbot and a mentor is, in large part, the gap between stateless and persistent.

2.5 Can One Agent Do Everything? Multi-Agent Specialisation

Even with memory, there is a coordination problem. Diagnosing what a student does not understand, designing a remediation plan, adapting the difficulty of the next problem, and evaluating whether the intervention worked are genuinely distinct tasks — each requiring different priorities and a different kind of attention. Collapsing them into a single agent produces something mediocre at each. The solution is the same one human institutions have used for centuries: divide the labour.

Wang et al. (2025b) formalise this as a five-agent architecture: a Gap Identifier diagnoses knowledge deficits from interaction data; a Learner Profiler maintains a model of the student’s characteristics and pace; a Dynamic Planner generates a personalised learning path; a Content Adapter calibrates difficulty and modality in real time; and a Feedback Synthesiser aggregates assessment signals into a coherent picture the other agents can act on. Think less “one very clever tutor” and more “a well-coordinated teaching team running in parallel.”

The deeper question underneath the architecture is: how does the system actually know what a student knows? Static test scores are a blunt instrument. Bayesian Knowledge Tracing (BKT) models each skill as a hidden binary state — known or unknown — updated via observed responses. Deep Knowledge Tracing (DKT) replaces this binary state with a continuous latent representation learned by an LSTM, capturing richer inter-skill dependencies. The most recent work goes further: Scarlatos et al. (2025) use the tutoring dialogue itself as the assessment instrument, refining knowledge estimates through conversation rather than formal tests. A well-designed agentic tutor is always assessing — even when it appears to simply be talking.

2.6 Can It Actually Run? Deployment, Compression, and the Two-Second Wall

Every component described above assumes access to a capable model running with acceptable latency. That assumption breaks quickly in real classrooms. State-of-the-art LLMs require GPU memory far beyond the Chromebooks and shared tablets that most students use. And even if the hardware exists, a voice-based tutor that pauses for more than two seconds between turns kills the conversational rhythm entirely. Deployment is not a downstream concern — it is a precondition for the system working at all.

The first layer of the solution is model compression. Quantization reduces each weight from 32-bit floating point to 4 or 8 bits via a learned scale factor \(S\) and zero-point offset \(Z\):

$$ W_q = ext{round}\!\left( rac{W_{ ext{fp}}}{S} ight) + Z $$

Calibration-aware methods like AWQ and GPTQ select \(S\) and \(Z\) to minimise task-specific accuracy loss. 4-bit quantized models retain over 91% benchmark accuracy while cutting memory footprint by up to 8× (Agrawal et al., 2025).

Knowledge distillation trains a compact student model to mimic a large teacher, recovering ~85% of capability at a fraction of the cost. Structured pruning removes attention heads and feed-forward layers that contribute least to output quality, shrinking models by up to 60% without retraining (Cai et al., 2026). Together, these make a capable tutor runnable on classroom hardware. But runnable is not the same as fast enough.

The latency problem has a different shape. End-to-end response time decomposes as:

$$ T_{\text{total}} = T_{\text{edge}}^{\text{infer}} + T_{\text{net}}^{\text{tx}} + T_{\text{cloud}}^{\text{infer}} + T_{\text{net}}^{\text{rx}} $$

Each term is a distinct engineering target. Edge inference is minimised by quantization and pruning. Network transmission is minimised by sending compact feature vectors rather than raw audio. Cloud inference — the dominant cost — is where speculative decoding intervenes.

Edge-cloud co-design manages the architecture: privacy-sensitive, latency-critical tasks (speech detection, engagement scoring, audio feature extraction) run on the student’s device; heavy reasoning, RAG retrieval, and agent orchestration run in the cloud. Transmitting compact feature vectors rather than raw waveforms compresses the upstream payload by an order of magnitude — directly cutting the most volatile part of the latency budget. Speculative decoding then handles the remaining bottleneck on the cloud side. Autoregressive generation is memory-bound: each token requires a full forward pass that loads billions of weights, leaving most GPU compute idle between steps. Speculative decoding pairs a small draft model with the large target model. The draft proposes a candidate sequence; the target verifies the entire sequence in a single parallel forward pass, accepting the correct prefix and regenerating from the first mismatch. When the draft model is accurate — which for predictable educational language it frequently is — multiple tokens are committed per step, raising throughput 2–3× with no change in output quality. PicoSpec (2025) implements this across an edge-cloud boundary with asynchronous pipelining, achieving up to 2.9× speedup on Llama-70B. Zhu et al. (2025) demonstrate 44.9% latency reduction through joint optimisation of speculation length and wireless resource allocation. The two-second wall is now an engineering specification with known solutions.

Part 3

The Hard Questions We Can’t Afford to Ignore

TL;DR

Four risks need design-level responses, not just policy. Privacy can be guaranteed architecturally through edge computing. Cognitive dependency is countered by Socratic scaffolding. The digital divide (2.6B still offline) demands infrastructure investment alongside AI deployment. And academic integrity benefits more from prevention-by-design than post-hoc detection. The EU AI Act now classifies educational AI as "high-risk."

3.1 Your Child’s Learning Data: Who Sees It, and What Happens Next?

To personalise effectively, an AI tutor needs to know a lot about a student: what they struggle with, how quickly they learn, when they get frustrated, what motivates them. When the student is eight years old, that data profile raises questions that go well beyond GDPR, FERPA, and COPPA compliance.

The tension is structural: the richer the learner model, the better the tutoring, but the more dangerous a data breach or misuse becomes. A detailed profile of a child’s misconceptions, emotional patterns, and behavioural tendencies could, if compromised, enable discrimination in future education or employment. The 2025 systematic review of AI in classroom management found that only 22% of 104 studies even addressed ethical concerns. A striking gap given the sensitivity of the data involved. UNESCO (2025) warns that without equitable safeguards, AI-powered education risks deepening the inequalities it claims to solve.

The EU AI Act, which classifies educational AI as “high-risk,” establishes requirements for transparency, human oversight, and data governance. But regulation alone is not enough. Privacy-by-design principles — data minimisation, purpose limitation, algorithmic explainability — need to be baked into the architecture itself.

And here is where the engineering from Part 2 pays an unexpected dividend. The edge computing architectures designed for latency also provide a physical guarantee of privacy. When speech recognition, facial engagement detection, and preliminary NLP run on-device, the most sensitive biometric data — a child’s voice, facial expressions, real-time behavioural patterns — never leaves the local hardware. No legal framework is as leak-proof as data that never touches a server.

3.2 The Cognitive Crutch Problem: Are We Thinking Less?

This might be the most uncomfortable question in the entire debate. Gerlich (2025) studied 666 participants and found a clear pattern: the more frequently people used AI tools, the worse they scored on critical thinking measures, with cognitive offloading — the externalisation of thinking to digital tools — as the mediating mechanism. Younger participants showed the strongest effect. A separate study in Acta Psychologica reached similar conclusions among university students.

Unrestricted ChatGPT access during self-directed study produced a steeper forgetting curve compared to unassisted learning — reduced effort during encoding leads to weaker memory consolidation.

Lee et al. (2024) formalise this as a guidance-based framework where the AI provides scaffolding that still requires the student to do the cognitive heavy lifting. The key insight from the literature is clear: it is the pedagogical design of AI tutors, not their raw capability, that determines whether they sharpen or erode the minds they are meant to serve.

3.3 2.6 Billion People Are Still Offline. AI Could Widen the Gap.

AI tutoring could be the great equaliser — or the great amplifier. As of 2024, 2.6 billion people remain offline. Internet access reaches 83% in urban areas but just 48% in rural ones (ITU, 2024). The divide is not just connectivity: it is AI literacy, linguistic inclusion, and cultural relevance. Most AI educational tools are built in English, for English-speaking contexts, with limited accommodation for the multilingual and indigenous communities that need them most (Frontiers in Computer Science, 2026).

Brookings (2024) articulated what they call the “new digital divide”: affluent students get AI tools plus human teachers who coach them on how to use those tools effectively. Disadvantaged students get the technology alone, without the scaffolding to make it educationally productive. The implication is sobering: simply distributing AI tutors without investing in teacher training, multilingual models, culturally responsive content, and basic infrastructure could make inequality worse, not better.

3.4 Cheating, Detection, and Why Prevention Beats Policing

Students will use AI to cheat. That is not a prediction; it is already happening. The question is what to do about it. AI-detection tools — the ones that try to identify machine-generated text — are unreliable. They produce both false positives and false negatives, and their accuracy degrades as models improve. Khanmigo takes a fundamentally different approach: architectural prevention. It structurally cannot generate complete essays, and it flags anything longer than fifteen words pasted from external sources. Rather than playing cat-and-mouse with detectors, it removes the opportunity in the first place.

Institutions are catching up. The most thoughtful universities now distinguish between prohibited, permitted, and encouraged uses of AI — recognising that a blanket ban is neither enforceable nor educationally productive. The strongest frameworks treat AI literacy as a learning objective in its own right: teaching students to use these tools critically and transparently rather than pretending the tools do not exist. Assessment redesign — oral examinations, process-based evaluation, in-class supervised tasks — creates contexts where AI assistance is either unnecessary or openly integrated. The goal is not to defeat AI. It is to make the learning itself the point.

Conclusions

The Two-Sigma Problem, Forty Years Later

In 1984, Benjamin Bloom proved that one-on-one tutoring could lift student performance by two standard deviations. The result was electrifying, and then, for four decades, frustrating. Everyone knew personalised tutoring worked. Nobody could afford to provide it. For the first time, the convergence of large language models, agentic architectures, retrieval-augmented generation, multi-agent systems, and multimodal perception offers a credible technological pathway to that goal. Millions of students are already interacting with AI tutors daily.

But the evidence in this blog points to a critical qualification that should temper any utopian impulse: hybrid human-AI workflows consistently outperform fully autonomous systems. The most effective implementations (Khanmigo, LPITutor, multi-agent educational frameworks) position AI as a tool for teachers and students, not a replacement for the relationships at the heart of education.

Teachers do not merely transmit information. They inspire curiosity, model intellectual integrity, navigate the emotional complexities of learning, and socialise young people into communities of practice. These functions are not replicable by current AI systems, however sophisticated.

Three open research questions will shape what comes next. First, cross-lingual equity: rigorous development and auditing of AI tutors in underrepresented languages, using participatory design that incorporates local pedagogical traditions. Second, longitudinal evidence: most existing evaluations span fewer than eight weeks; we need sustained, large-scale studies to understand long-term effects on learning, motivation, and cognitive development. Third, governance that keeps pace: AI deployment in education is outrunning the regulatory institutions meant to oversee it, and international coordination through frameworks like the EU AI Act and UNESCO’s Ethics of AI Recommendation will be essential.

The intelligent classroom is no longer speculative. It is emerging. The question is not whether AI will transform education (it already is) but whether that transformation will be guided by the values of equity, agency, and genuine understanding, or by commercial imperatives and technological momentum alone.

The promise of agentic AI in education is not that it will make teachers obsolete. It is that it will make the best practices of teaching (individualised attention, Socratic dialogue, formative assessment, adaptive scaffolding) available at a scale that was previously impossible. Bloom’s two-sigma problem may finally have a solution. But walking that path responsibly demands investment not only in technology, but in the institutional, ethical, and deeply human capital that gives education its meaning.
